Deep talk with AI

The Effects of Need for Cognition on Deep vs. Shallow Conversations with Artificial Agents
Master Thesis, 2020

This study analyzed how the depth of conversation affects the individual evaluation of interactions with AI by assigning 89 participants to either a deep talk or a shallow talk with a chatbot.

It also investigated whether this individual engagement and evaluation of interactions with these different conversational agents differs between individuals with a high vs. low level of need for cognition (NFC).

Three dimensions were compared:
(1) number of questions participants answered
(2) length of responses
(3) evaluation.

Results suggest that deep conversation is generally more likely to arouse longer responses and high opinion for the chatbot (Figure 1&2), and people who score low in NFC enjoy the deep talk better than people who are high in NFC. (Figure 2&3)

In contrast to the speculation, the findings imply that the deep talk, rather than the shallow talk, is more appealing to low-NFC individuals: Low-NFC participants were more satisfied with the deep talk than the shallow talk, while for high-NFC individuals, the evaluation of the deep talk is on average even lower than the shallow talk.

A possible reason supported by results for this deviation from the assumptions based on NFC theories is that low-NFC individuals feel understood more easily during the conversation with chatbots, leading to more positive attitudes toward the chat experience, whereas high-NFC individuals are inclined to prefer communication with humans and are unlikely to feel understood or perceive a talk with chatbots as “deep”.

As the observations indicate, the deepness was perceived especially when people felt understood by the chatbot, and their evaluation is highly related to these factors as well. (Figure 4&6)

This phenomenon is aligned with the observations of NFC research that simple pre-defined artificial conversational agents could fulfill communicative needs of low-NFC individuals more easily, who handle information roughly because they are not so keen on thinking hard. (Figure 5&7)

But chatbots could hardly satisfy high-NFC people, who are adept at understanding abstract verbal information and process thoroughly and critically, they require the bots to reply to their messages like intelligent humans.

Just as some participants expressed their disappointment in the feedback, they figured out the responses from the chatbot were pre-defined, so it could not converse with true understanding, or the empathy it showed was actually fake.

Therefore, considering individual personalities, people who are low in NFC are expected to be more willing to accept and enjoy talking with conversational agents than people high in NFC.
